EduVital is a dedicated platform designed to support students in managing their mental health and academic well-being. My goal for this project is to help students thrive both personally and academically, fostering resilience and self-care in a supportive online space.

About

This section aims to align and inform people about my objectives of the study

1. Develop a user-friendly web interface that provides access to mental health resources, tools, and support services.

2. Implement features within the application that promote stress management techniques, relaxation exercises, and mindfulness practices.

3. Create a secure and confidential platform for students to seek guidance from licensed mental health professionals through chat or virtual counseling sessions.

4. Collaborate with university counseling centers and mental health experts to ensure the accuracy, relevance, and effectiveness of the resources and interventions offered within.

5. Evaluate the usability, effectiveness, and impact of the website through user feedback, surveys, and qualitative assessments to identify areas for improvement and further development.

Kowalski et al. (2023) examined the relationship between social media use and depression in young adults. They found a positive correlation between increased social media usage and higher rates of depression symptoms.

Twenge et al. (2022) observed a significant increase in depression and anxiety symptoms among U.S. college students over the past decade, coinciding with increased smartphone ownership and social media use.

Andrews et al. (2023) conducted a randomized controlled trial evaluating the efficacy of a mobile app-based cognitive-behavioral therapy intervention for stress management in college students. The results showed promising outcomes in reducing stress levels and improving coping mechanisms.

Johnson et al. (2023) surveyed college students to investigate the relationship between social media use and self-esteem, suggesting a negative correlation between excessive social media use and self-esteem.

Chen et al. (2022) explored the experiences of college students using digital mental health apps, identifying several factors influencing adoption and adherence to these platforms.

Smith et al. (2023) investigated the role of peer support groups within digital mental health platforms for college students, highlighting the importance of community support in enhancing treatment outcomes.

Lee et al. (2023) conducted a systematic review on the effectiveness of gamification elements in digital mental health interventions for young adults, indicating that incorporating game-like features could enhance engagement and motivation in online therapy sessions.

Brown et al. (2022) at Harvard University examined the impact of personalized feedback on mental health outcomes in a digital platform for college students, showing significant improvements in self-reported well-being and coping skills.

The American College Health Association (ACHA) nationwide survey in 2023 revealed alarming rates of mental health issues among college students, highlighting the need for accessible and effective digital mental health interventions.

Patel et al. (2023) explored the implementation of a digital mental health platform in a large public university, providing insights into the practical challenges and successes of integrating technology-based support into traditional campus mental health services.

Kim et al. (2023) compared the effectiveness of group versus individual online therapy sessions for college students struggling with anxiety, suggesting that group therapy might be more cost-effective while maintaining comparable efficacy.

Chang et al. (2023) at Stanford University evaluated the impact of mindfulness-based digital interventions on stress reduction and academic performance in college students, observing significant improvements in both areas.

Hall et al. (2023) tracked the mental health trajectories of college students over four years, observing changes in symptom severity and treatment-seeking behaviors in relation to shifts in digital technology use.

Wong et al. (2023) conducted focus group research exploring college students' perceptions of privacy and security concerns when using digital mental health platforms, highlighting the need for clear communication about data protection measures.

Rodriguez et al. (2023) examined the role of artificial intelligence in digital mental health interventions for college students, suggesting that AI-powered chatbots could potentially enhance accessibility and personalization of support.
